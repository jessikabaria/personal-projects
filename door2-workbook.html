From the Deck — The Expert Trap
When Expertise Impedes Growth
Being skilled and respected is valuable. But expertise becomes a trap when it starts protecting the identity instead of serving the work.
Type 1
Avoids new challenges to protect their track record.
Type 2
Only takes on work they're already confident they'll succeed at.
Type 3
Frames resistance to change as experience, caution, or wisdom.
None of these people think of themselves as having a fixed mindset. They think of themselves as being careful, experienced, or realistic. That's what makes the Expert Trap so effective — it disguises itself as wisdom.
2 Minutes
Rewrite the Expert Trap
Step 1
Write your Expert Trap statement — the fixed mindset version:
I'm not... I can't... We don't... That's not how we...
Step 2
Rewrite it using the Power of Yet:
I haven't... yet. We haven't found a way... yet.
"Yet" doesn't pretend the gap isn't real. It changes your relationship to the gap. The gap becomes a destination, not a verdict.

Principle 1 — Raise Your Own Bar Ambition over comfort
  • Did I choose a goal that was safe and easy because I knew I wouldn't fail?
  • Did I do just enough to get a task passed, instead of pushing to see how much better it could be?
  • Did I avoid a harder assignment because I worried about making a mistake in front of my team?
  • Did I accept a slow timeline just because it felt more comfortable than rushing?
Principle 2 — Turn Action Into Traction Outputs over effort
  • Did I spend a day in back-to-back meetings but finish no real work?
  • Did I spend hours formatting a spreadsheet or polishing a deck just to look impressive?
  • Did I feel exhausted at day's end, but realize I didn't actually ship or launch anything?
  • Did I spend more time talking about my work than actually doing it?
Principle 3 — Thrive in Change Shifts as opportunities
  • Did I spend time complaining about a change, reorg, or new rule instead of dealing with it?
  • Did I pause a project because a small requirement changed, rather than adjusting and moving forward?
  • Did I wait for step-by-step instructions instead of making a move myself?
  • Did I catch myself wishing a process or team structure would go back to the old way?
Principle 4 — Move Fast, Simplify, Repeat Urgency, cutting bloat
  • Did I follow a heavy, slow process simply because "that's how we've always done it"?
  • Did I invite a large group to a meeting just to avoid making a tough decision alone?
  • Did I delay a project waiting for 100% perfect data before making a move?
  • Did I make a simple problem more complicated by adding extra review steps or approvals?
From the Deck — The Door 2 Standard
You Oscillate. That Is Normal.
The standard is not perfection — it is what you do with it. Growth mindset is not a binary switch. It is a spectrum with three states you move between multiple times a day.
Fixed Mindset
Catch
yourself when you default to comfort.
Passive Growth Mindset
Bypass
the passive plateau — don't just intend to act.
Active Growth Mindset
Leap
into active execution — even when the moment is imperfect.
Example: A Principal Engineer might be a fierce Active Growth practitioner when optimising code architecture — and drop into defensive fixed mindset when a cross-functional meeting threatens their timeline.

Partner Up — The Red Pencil Fix
Test each other's plans. Reject soft words and rewrite them sharp.
Soft-Edged Words — Reject These
"I will be more decisive."
Describes an attitude, not an action.
"I will simplify our reporting."
Doesn't say which report disappears or who's affected.
"I will try to adapt better."
Soft verbs allow excuses when timelines slip.
Sharp-Edged Words — Rewrite to These
"I will make [decision] by Friday at 2:00 PM."
Defines the boundary and the timeline.
"I am eliminating the weekly status report deck."
Names the exact report being terminated.
"I am adapting the approach by [adjustment] by [date]."
Gives a verifiable definition of done.

"An action plan in a closed notebook is useless. Pick one concrete action and execute within 72 hours."

Line 1 — The Action
What is the exact task you will cut, simplify, or execute?
Line 2 — The Deadline
The exact day and time within the next 72 hours.
Line 3 — The Evidence
The exact visible proof that shows the step is complete.

Growth Mindset x Door 2 Strategies
How to live the four principles every day.
Raise Your Own Bar
Choose Challenge Over Comfort
The Trap
Staying in your comfort zone, avoiding risks, and doing just enough to get by safely without making mistakes.
The Strategy
Actively take on difficult, more ambiguous responsibilities to test your limits and deliver more.
Turn Action Into Traction
Focus Heavily on Outcomes
The Trap
Measuring your value by how busy you look, how many meetings you attend, or how much performative effort you put in.
The Strategy
Continually audit your work. Ask if what you are doing right now is finishing a goal or creating busywork.
Thrive in Change
Treat Surprises as Data
The Trap
Getting frustrated by shifts, being defensive when plans alter, or pausing to wait for perfect step-by-step instructions.
The Strategy
View sudden changes or mistakes as learning opportunities to pivot, adapt, and improve your approach.
Move Fast. Simplify. Repeat.
Ruthlessly Strip Away Friction
The Trap
Accepting heavy, slow, or over-complicated workflows simply because "that's how we have always done it here."
The Strategy
Eliminate unnecessary steps, administrative bloat, and operational bottlenecks that slow down speed.
Not There YET! Mistakes are data. They mean you're on the right track — just not there yet.
